The Maltese Falcon, As I Lay Dying, and Miss Marple are now free to read

On January 1, everything published in 1930 entered the U.S. public domain. According to Standard Ebooks, the volunteer-run project prepared 20 new free ebooks to celebrate, including some major titles: Dashiell Hammett's The Maltese Falcon, William Faulkner's As I Lay Dying, Franz Kafka's The Castle, and Agatha Christie's The Murder at the Vicarage — the first full-length Miss Marple novel.

Other highlights: Langston Hughes' semi-autobiographical Not Without Laughter from the Harlem Renaissance. Evelyn Waugh's satirical Vile Bodies. Dorothy L. Sayers' Strong Poison from the Lord Peter Wimsey series. Arthur Ransome's children's adventure Swallows and Amazons. And the original 1930 Nancy Drew mysteries, before decades of revisions sanitized them.

The Kafka entry is particularly significant. The 1930 English translation of The Castle introduced Kafka to English-speaking audiences — his work had been largely unknown outside German until then. That translation is now free to read, share, and build upon.

Under the original 1790 copyright law, works received 28 years of protection. These books would have entered the public domain in 1958. Instead, after multiple extensions — most notoriously the 1998 Sonny Bono Act, pushed by Disney to protect Mickey Mouse — they waited 95 years. Standard Ebooks and similar projects exist because public domain books cost less, appear in more formats, and stay in print longer than copyrighted works. The 95-year wait is finally over for 1930.
