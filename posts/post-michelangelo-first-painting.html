A 12-year-old Michelangelo painted this, but no one knew for 500 years

Before the Sistine Chapel ceiling, before the David, before any of the masterworks that would make him a legend, a preteen Michelangelo was painting demons. The Torment of Saint Anthony shows the desert hermit besieged by grotesque creatures — and the kid who painted it was maybe 12 or 13 years old.

The painting spent five centuries as a nobody's work until a buyer brought it to the Metropolitan Museum of Art in 2008 for cleaning. Conservators scraped away the grime and found something unexpected: a color palette and approach to human figures that looked a lot like the Sistine Chapel. Infrared imaging revealed pentimenti — correction marks showing the artist was making creative decisions, not just copying someone else's work.

The Kimbell Art Museum in Fort Worth took the leap, with its director finding "not a single convincing argument against the attribution." Art historian Giorgio Bonsanti later backed up the claim, reports Open Culture.

It's now the only Michelangelo painting in the Americas and one of just four easel paintings attributed to him — fitting, since he later trash-talked oil painting as a medium. Some scholars remain skeptical, and Michelangelo himself probably would have dismissed it as juvenilia. But there's something undeniably compelling about seeing a future master's work from when he was still young enough to be grounded for staying out too late.
