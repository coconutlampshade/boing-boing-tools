Rural communities once put frogs in milk to keep it from spoiling

Before refrigerators, rural communities in Russia and Finland faced a problem: how to keep milk from spoiling. Their solution was to drop a live brown frog (Rana temporaria) into the jar. This practice, common in the 19th and early 20th centuries, was passed down through generations. People observed that milk stayed fresh longer with a frog present, though they didn't understand why. I wouldn't recommend trying it today, but Got Weird on Instagram has the science.

Modern research has vindicated the folklore. A 2012-2013 study led by Dr. Albert Lebedev at Moscow State University found that brown frog skin secretes potent antimicrobial peptides — natural antibiotic compounds that inhibit bacteria and fungi. These compounds, including one called Brevinin 1Tb, proved effective against harmful bacteria like Staphylococcus and Salmonella. The frog was essentially acting as nature's preservative.

It's a reminder that folk wisdom sometimes has real biochemistry behind it — an ingenious, if unwitting, application of natural science that helped people preserve a vital food source before technology caught up.
