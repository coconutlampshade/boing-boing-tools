The Nazi plan to destroy Britain's economy by dropping forged banknotes from planes

In 1940, the Nazis hatched a scheme so audacious it sounds like rejected Bond villain plotting: flood Britain with forged banknotes dropped from aircraft, triggering hyperinflation and economic collapse. The operation, codenamed Unternehmen Andreas (later Operation Bernhard), was run by the Sicherheitsdienst, the SS intelligence service.

The technical achievement was genuinely impressive. German forgers successfully duplicated the rag paper used in British currency, produced near-identical engraving blocks, and — here's the kicker — reverse-engineered the algorithm the Bank of England used to generate serial numbers. Each fake note had a unique, valid-looking serial code.

The initial air-drop plan was eventually abandoned, but the operation continued. Concentration camp prisoners with backgrounds in printing and banking were forced to produce the forgeries. By war's end, they'd manufactured around £132 million in fake notes — roughly £6 billion in today's money. Some of this funny money actually entered circulation, used to pay German agents and buy supplies.

The whole enterprise collapsed in early 1942 when its head, Alfred Naujocks, fell out of favor with Reinhard Heydrich. The Bank of England responded by withdrawing all notes over £5 from circulation, a policy that lasted until 1945. Wikipedia has the full story of this bonkers economic warfare attempt.
