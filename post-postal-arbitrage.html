Why mail a postcard when you can Prime someone a lime?

A U.S. postage stamp costs 78 cents. A lime on Amazon Prime costs 25 cents and arrives in two days with free shipping. Developer Ryan Walz noticed this pricing inefficiency and turned it into what he calls "postal arbitrage" — sending physical gifts through Amazon instead of traditional mail.

The trick is finding Prime-eligible items under the cost of a stamp. Walz compiled a list: Kool-Aid packets for 42 cents, pasta for 50 cents, hurricane tie hardware for 51 cents, potatoes for 56 cents, tomato sauce for 70 cents. You add a gift note with your message and Amazon handles the rest.

It started in 2023 when Walz sent $1 cans of beans to extended family. The move sparked weeks of sustained group chat engagement. Then things escalated. Family members started sending each other random items — an asbestos warning label, cookies, a tin of sardines, and for reasons left unexplained, a pregnancy test.

The economics only work because Amazon has built a logistics network so efficient that delivering a single lime to someone's door costs them less than what the USPS charges to move a half-ounce piece of paper. That's either impressive or dystopian, depending on your feelings about warehouse working conditions. But Walz's argument is hard to dispute: "You're not only saving money. It's about sending something real." Your aunt might throw away a postcard. She'll probably eat the potato.
